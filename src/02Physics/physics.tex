\section{Goals and Methods}
\emph{Physics} is an activity of \emph{human community} pursuing the following major goals: \emph{Describe}, \emph{explain}, and \emph{predict} phenomena comprising the observed world. These goals might not seem lofty enough since they say nothing about the search for an absolute truth, the ultimate origins of the universe, or the true nature of reality. Admittedly, description, explanation, and prediction imply that someone is performing these activities and someone is benefiting from them. However, we must keep in mind how important the human element in physics is. The true nature of physical reality, if uncovered, will still be communicated by physicists to other \emph{people} in a human language, with the hope that the result will be appreciated and a noble use for new knowledge will be found.

Description and explanation can be beautiful, satisfying, and valuable but the real power of physics comes from its \emph{predictive ability}. Prediction is the ultimate test of any theory. 

How do physicist achieve those goals?

\section{Classical and Quantum}
Classical and quantum physics have many similarities. For example, both employ such basic ideas as: \emph{system, state, dynamics, interaction, measurement, observer, information, probability}. These concepts do not describe any specific phenomena or intstruments, but rather represent elements of a framework used to \emph{think the world}.

To a student, the similarities might make quantum physics seem less foreign, but they also may hinder the understanding of truly and purely quantum features. The more we learn and understand quantum physics, the clearer it becomes that the \emph{meaning} of the basic concepts is often different.

The interconnection between the classical and quantum physics goes beyond shared concepts and mathematical tools. Some physicists pointed out that quantum physics \emph{can't stand on its own}.

\begin{mybio}{Lev Landau On Classical and Quantum}
	Deep connection between the classical and quantum physics is emphasized by Lev Landau in the third volume of theoretical physics "Quantum Mechanics":
	
	"
	Usually a more general theory can be formulated in a way logically independent from the less general limiting case. Thus, relativistic mechanics can be formulated based on its own principles, without any reference to Newtonian mechanics. The basic assumptions of quantum mechanics, though, fundamentally can't be formulated without the reference to classical mechanics.
	"
	
	{\it Quantum Mechanics: Non-relativistic theory, Chapter 1, Section 1}
\end{mybio}

\section{System}\label{sec:System}
A part of nature that can be clearly isolated and studied is called a \emph{physical system}. An electron, an atom, a molecule, a crystal, a pendulum, a comet, a star – these are examples of physical systems of various degrees of complexity.

In quantum physics the concept of a system is expanded to include not only "physical objects" but also some \emph{aspects of their behavior}. Thus, for the same physical entity (e.g. mode of electromagnetic field), we can focus on its polarization and treat it as a quantum system, disregarding its behavior in time and space. In other words, a separate \emph{degree of freedom} can be viewed as quantum system. Another example is the spin of an electron trapped inside a tiny volume. Electrons have special degree of freedom called \emph{spin}. If quantum experiment focuses on manipulating spin, then the latter can be treated as a distinct quantum system.

\section{State}
\emph{State} is a very important concept in physics. State of a system means \emph{complete} but \emph{minimal} knowledge about values of its various parameters and system's possible behavior.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[scale=1.0]{stateAsKnowledge}
	\caption{State is a minimal and complete knowledge about a physical system.}
	\label{fig:stateAsKnowledge}
\end{figure}
By \emph{minimal} knowledge we mean that if position of particle $x$ is known, there is no need to know $x^3$ or any other one-to-one function of position. We only need to know and keep track of the \emph{essential} information. 

As we'll see, mathematically state can be represented by a number, a pair of numbers, a function, or some other mathematical object, depending on a specific theory. If we denote some such mathematical representation of a state as $K$, then the completeness of a state can be symbolically expressed as a relation:
\[
K_0\, \overset{f}{\xrightarrow{\hspace*{2cm}}}\, v\,.
\]
This expression should be read as follows: Given a state $K$ at moment of time $t_0$, one can calculate the value $v$ of any meaningful physical parameter.

\begin{myExample}
	In Newtonian mechanics the state of a system is given by a pair "position and velocity": $K=(x, v)$. Consider, for instance, a body with mass $m$ attached to a spring with the spring constant $k$. From $K$ we can calculate the body's momentum $p=mv$, kinetic energy $E_k=mv^2/2$, and the potential energy of the spring: $E_p=kx^2/2$.
\end{myExample}

\begin{mybio}{Completeness}
	How can we be sure that the information about a system is complete? Can there be some "hidden" information, inaccessible (may be yet, or even potentially forever) to us and yet affecting the behavior of a system?
	
	The question of completeness is an important one. In the context of quantum physics it was raised for the first time by Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky, and Nathan Rosen in 1935.
\end{mybio}

\section{Deterministic Evolution}
The completeness of a state is a very strong constraint. Not only it means \emph{"everything there is to know at a given moment"}, but also \emph{"know state now -- know state always."} The latter is an expression of \emph{determinism}: the complete knowledge of a system is fully determined at all times once an initial state is known. However, state by itself  is not sufficient to satisfy the latter requirement, it must be supplemented by the so called \emph{dynamical equations}. These equations are specific to a physical system and encapsulate the laws that govern internal interactions. EXAMPLE?

Determinism can be expressed as the following relation:

\[
K_0\, \overset{U}{\xrightarrow{\hspace*{2cm}}}\, K_1\,,
\]
Here $K_0$ and $K_1$ are the states at the moments of time $t_0$ and $t_1$, respectively. The function $U$ calculates the state $K_1$ from the state $K_0$. It is sometimes said the the function $U$ \emph{evolves} the state $K_0$ into the state $K_t$, and the function $U$ is called the \emph{evolution function}.

\begin{myExample}
	For a uniform circular motion the state is simply the angle $\phi$, and the evolution is given by a simple formula
	\[
	\phi_1 =\omega (t_1-t_0) + \phi_0\,.	
	\]
	Notice that in this case the evolution function $U$ depends on the time \emph{difference} $t_1-t_0$ and not on each moment of time separately.
\end{myExample}

\begin{myExample}
	In Newtonian mechanics the state of a body is given by the pair $K=(x, v)$. For a body in free fall the state changes according to the equations
	\[
	v_1 = v_0 + g(t_1-t_0)\,,
	\]
	\[
	x_1 = x_0 + v_0(t_1-t_0)+g(t_1-t_0)^2/2\,.
	\]
	Notice again, that the evolution function $U$ depends on the time \emph{difference} $t_1-t_0$ and not on each moment of time separately. Of course, in this case we have two evolution functions, one for the position, and the other for velocity.
\end{myExample}

\section{Measurement}
State of a system is closely related to the \emph{knowledge} of its possible behavior. Where does that knowledge come from? \emph{Door meten tot weten} or \emph{Through measurement to knowing} as dutch experimental physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes said. He received 1913 Nobel Prize in physics "for his investigations on the properties of matter at low temperatures which led, inter alia, to the production of liquid helium."

In physics the primary source of quantitative knowledge comes from experiments. Measurement is the source of our knowledge about the world. This is true for both classical and quantum physics. However, the role measurement plays in classical physics differs substantially from the role it plays in quantum physics. Let's examine the idea and process of measurement closer.

\section{Atoms}
Classical physics, mainly classical mechanics and classical electrodynamics, describes atoms as unstable configuration of charges and predicts their decay in an "atomic heartbeat" (nanosecond). There should be no stable atoms according to classical physics, and the reason for all atoms having the same size is not clear at all.

What is observed is, firstly, stable configuration of positive and negative charges in every atom and, secondly,  spontaneous decay of atoms from excited state into non-excited state. Quantum physics elegantly explains the latter.  

\section{Particles}
Modern understanding of the particle concept is significantly more nuanced than simple "point-like object with certain physical attributes". Instead, particles are defined based on quantum behavior of corresponding \emph{physical fields}. Quantum behavior of electromagnetic field corresponds to photons, and quantum behavior of electron-positron field corresponds to electrons (and their anti-particles).

\begin{myrem}{Particles}
	Particles are excitations of quantum fields. They correspond to irreducible unitary representations of Lorentz group. Fields are operator-valued vector functions defined over Minkowski space and transformed simply under the action of the Poincare group.
\end{myrem}


\subsection{Photons And Electrons}

\section{Single Particle Detectors}
Detecting a single particle is a challenging task. The first time humans distinctly observed a single particle was in 1908 in an experiment performed by Rutherford and Geiger. 

\subsection{Rutherford-Geiger Method}\label{subsec:RutherformGeiger}
Rutherford and Geiger described their results in the paper \emph{"An Electrical Method of Counting the Number of alpha-Particles from Radio-active Substances"}. The idea of their experiment, schematically illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:RutherfordGeigerExperiment},  is as follows: Place a small amount of radioactive substance in vacuum and detect an effect of an extremely narrow "beam" or "ray" of radiation going in a specified direction. The effect Rutherford and Geiger used on to detect a single particle is based on \emph{avalanche amplification} of electron currents in a specially designed detector.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[scale=1.0]{RutherfordGeigerExperiment}
	\caption{(a) Schematic of the Rutherford-Geiger experiment for detecting a single subatomic particle. (b) Current from the amplifier at various moments of time.}
	\label{fig:RutherfordGeigerExperiment}
\end{figure}

\begin{mybio}{Amplification}
	The avalanche amplification of the effects caused by a single subatomic particle is just one illustration of an important feature of observations and measurements of quantum systems: \emph{"the amplification of microscopic effects to the level of macroscopic observation,"}\footnote{{\it Quantum Kinematics and Dynamics}, Addendum {\it Non-selective measurements}, reproduced from the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences Vol 1. 45. pp. 1552-1553 (1959)} to borrow the phrase of an American theoretical physicist and Nobel Prize winner Julian Seymour Schwinger.
	
	The amplification, in one form or another, is necessary if we want to perform and discuss any quantum experiment. As Niels Bohr put it:
	
	\emph{"Here, it must above all be recognized that, however far  quantum effects transcend the  scope of  classical physical analysis, the account of the experimental arrangement and  the  record of  the  observations must always be
		expressed in common language supplemented with the
		terminology of  classical physics.  This  is  a  simple logical demand, since the word "experiment" can in
		essence be used only in referring to a situation where we  can tell  others what we  have done and what we
		have learned."}\footnote{{\it On the Notions of  Causality and Complementarity}, Science, 1950, Vol. 111}
\end{mybio}


\subsubsection*{Numbers}
Rutherford and Geiger used radium as radioactive material. Radium atom contains 88 protons and 138 neutrons, with the total mass of the atom $m_{Ra}=3.77\times 10^{-22}$ gram. The sample of 1 gram would contain $N=10^{22}/3.33=2.65\times 10^{21}$ atoms. The most stable isotope of radium decays slowly, taking 1600 years for half of the atoms to decay. In one second a tiny fraction of  the half of gram would decay, specifically:
\[
N = \frac{2.65\times 10^{21}}{2}\times \frac{1}{1600\times 365\times 24\times 60 \times 60}=2.65\times 10^{10}\textrm{ atoms}.
\]
From their measurements, Rutherford and Geiger estimated that one gram of radium emits $N\approx 3.2\times 10^{10}$ alpha-particles.

If every atomic decay resulted in an electron going through a wire, then we would have a small current of 
\[
I = \frac{Q}{t} = \frac{N q_e}{t} = \frac{3\times 10^{10}\times 1.6\times 10^{-19}}{1}=4.8\times 10^{-9}\,\textrm{A}\approx 5\,\textrm{nA}\,.
\]
Such current is easily measureable by modern instruments. However, Rutherford and Geiger were using much smaller amount of radium (1/10000 of gram!) and had to amplify the current two thousand times, making it easier to detect and strong enough to make audible "clicks."
